# Takeover — Investor Pitch Script

> **Format:** 5 min pitch + 5 min live demo, sandwiched. Pitch opens (~3 min) → demo (~5 min) → pitch closes (~2 min). Total ~10 min, leaving ~5 min for Q&A in a typical 15-min slot.
>
> **Audience:** seed / pre-seed investors.
> **Speaker:** founder, single person on stage.
> **Tone:** confident, specific, no hand-waving. Every claim grounded in something visible.
> **Anti-patterns to avoid:** never say "AI-powered", "synergy", "platform" without a noun after it, "Uber for X", "10x", "disrupting". Investors have heard all of these.

---

- [ ] Laptop on stage power, charger plugged in
- [ ] Second screen / iPad set up showing the candidate's email inbox (for demo step 5)

# Part 1 — Opening slides (~3 min, before demo)

## Slide 1 — Title (0:00–0:15, 15s)

**Visual.** Just the wordmark. Black background. "TAKEOVER" in white, "the AI ops platform" small underneath, in muted red. Nothing else. Let it breathe for five seconds before you say a word.

**Script.**
> "Hi, I'm [name], CEO of CodeWithAli. In the next ten minutes I'm going to show you a working product that replaces fifteen of the SaaS tools your portfolio companies are paying for right now, and the head of operations who runs them."

**Note.** The opening sentence is doing a lot of work — claim, scope, and a slight provocation. Don't rush it. Pause after "right now."

---

## Slide 2 — The problem (0:15–0:45, 30s)

**Visual.** Logo grid of 15 SaaS at 60% opacity, arranged in 3 rows of 5. Above them, in white: **"This is what an ops team pays for every month."** Below them: **"$2,300+ per month. $30k+ per year."**

**Script.**
> "Every company between ten people and a thousand people runs on roughly this stack. An ATS. A scheduler. An e-signature tool. An HRIS. A chat app. A wiki. An automation tool. A bookkeeper. An invoicer. A CRM. None of them talk to each other. The thing that makes them talk is a human. That human is the most expensive employee in your portfolio companies, and they spend most of their day copy-pasting between tabs."

**Note.** Look at the audience, not the slide. They've all seen this slide before; the line that lands is the human-as-glue claim. Slow down on "the most expensive employee."

---

## Slide 3 — What we built (0:45–1:15, 30s)

**Visual.** Single screenshot of Takeover, the candidate inbox view, full bleed. Caption: **"Takeover. One app. One workflow. One agent."**

**Script.**
> "Takeover is one desktop app that replaces all of those tools. Think Notion's database model for the data, Zapier's workflow model for the automation, and an AI agent — we call him Axon — sitting on top of all of it with the authority to actually do work. Not summarize email. Not draft a memo. *Do work*. Send the offer, book the meeting, onboard the hire."

**Note.** "Notion + Zapier + an AI agent that does work" is the positioning sentence. If you only remember one line from this pitch, that's the one. Practice it until it sounds like you've said it a hundred times — because by demo day you will have.

---

## Slide 4 — The wedge (1:15–2:00, 45s)

**Visual.** Three-panel diagram. Left panel: stack of recruiting tool logos (Greenhouse, Calendly, DocuSign, BambooHR) with arrows between them and a stick-figure human in the middle. Caption: **"Today: 4 tools + 1 recruiter + 20 hours per hire."** Right panel: Takeover screenshot with Axon orb visible. Caption: **"Takeover: 1 tool + 0 recruiters + 30 minutes per hire."** Middle panel: an arrow labeled **"What AI is actually good for."**

**Script.**
> "We're going wide eventually, but we're going to win by going deep first. Our wedge is talent operations. Recruiting, scheduling, signing, onboarding, and the first ninety days of training a new hire. This is a category that pays Greenhouse and Lever something like three billion dollars a year for an inbox and a checklist. It's exactly the kind of work an LLM should do, and exactly the kind of work an LLM hasn't been able to do until now, because the missing piece was a trusted agent with action authority — not just text generation. That's the thing we built. Let me show you."

**Note.** "Trusted agent with action authority" is the differentiator from ChatGPT and Copilot. Lean into it. The handoff phrase "let me show you" should be your cue to switch from slides to the live app — don't say "demo" out loud, just transition.

---

## Slide 5 — Demo handoff (2:00–2:10, 10s)

**Visual.** Full-bleed Takeover screen, fading in as your laptop projects the app. No talking.

**Script.**
> *(silent — just bring up Takeover, click into the candidate inbox)*

**Note.** Silence here is intentional and powerful. The room watches you operate the product. The pitch becomes the demo.

# Part 2 — Live demo (2:10–7:10, ~5 min)

> Full step-by-step in `demo-runbook.md`. Don't memorize that file's exact words — memorize the *beats* and the *fallback lines*. The voice commands themselves should sound natural, not scripted.

**One-line preview of what the room sees:**
1. Twelve fresh applications open in front of you.
2. You ask Axon (out loud) to rank them. Three names rise to the top, ranked, with reasoning.
3. You ask Axon to schedule an interview. Calendly fires; confirmation appears.
4. You narrate: "let's say the interview went well." You ask Axon to make the offer.
5. Cut to second screen: candidate inbox, accept page, signature.
6. Cut back: Axon: "Sarah accepted. I've added her to the team chat, generated her thirty-sixty-ninety day plan, and scheduled her welcome for Monday."
7. New hire logs in. Axon greets her by name and starts the walkthrough — guided tour of the app by voice. End on the magic moment: Sarah asks a free-form question and Axon answers from company docs.

The closing line of the demo is the bridge back to the slides: **"That was twelve resumes, one hire, zero recruiter hours, in under five minutes."**

# Part 3 — Closing slides (~2 min, after demo)

## Slide 6 — The moat (7:10–7:55, 45s)

**Visual.** A flowchart: at the top, the Axon orb. From it, arrows fan out to the 31 actions in the registry (text labels: "send offer," "book interview," "onboard hire," "generate plan," "send invoice," "post journal entry," etc.). Caption at the bottom: **"Same agent. Every workflow."**

**Script.**
> "What you just watched isn't a hiring tool with an AI chat bolted on the side. It's a general-purpose AI agent with a hiring workflow plugged into it — and thirty other workflows plugged into the same agent. The same Axon that just hired Sarah also runs our chat, our invoicing, our books, our scheduling. Every workflow we add makes every other workflow more valuable, because the agent gets more context. That's the moat. Greenhouse can ship AI features. They can't ship Axon."

**Note.** This is the slide that justifies a higher valuation than a vertical recruiting tool would get. The defensibility line is "every workflow we add makes every other workflow more valuable." Say it slowly.

---

## Slide 7 — The 15 SaaS (7:55–8:25, 30s)

**Visual.** Same logo grid from slide 2, but now greyscale and visibly faded. Overlaid on top, in red: **"All of it. One app."** And in the corner, a small price: **"$2,300/mo → [TAKEOVER PRICE TBD]/mo."**

**Script.**
> "Today Takeover replaces thirteen of the fifteen tools on this slide for a typical ten-person company. The other two are on a clear roadmap. The headline number is two thousand three hundred dollars a month, which doesn't include the cost of the person who keeps the stack from falling over. We price ourselves to keep most of that savings on the customer's side of the table — because the real lock-in isn't price, it's data gravity. Every candidate, every offer, every invoice, every chat message lives in one schema. The longer they use us, the harder we are to leave."

**Note.** Bracketed pricing line — fill in what you actually plan to charge. Suggested anchoring: $99/user/mo, or $499/mo flat up to 25 seats. We can A/B in the deck.

---

## Slide 8 — Traction, team, ask (8:25–9:10, 45s)

**Visual.** Three columns.
- **Column 1: Traction.** "[X] active users daily" / "[Y] companies in pilot" / "[Z] hires processed end-to-end since launch" / one logo if you have a real customer
- **Column 2: Team.** Your face + title, plus 1–3 lines of "previously: [X], built [Y]"
- **Column 3: Ask.** "$[X] on $[Y] cap. [Z] months of runway. [N] hires."

**Script.**
> "On traction: [fill in honestly — even small numbers, but real ones. If pre-revenue, say "we've run our own company on this for [X] months and processed [Y] hires end-to-end" — that's a valid form of traction]. On team: I'm [name]. Before this I [one credible line]. We're raising [X] on [Y] post-money — that gets us to [milestone] and lets us hire [the two roles that matter most]."

**Note.** This is the slide I can't write for you. Three honest numbers and one credible team line will do more than any framing I could invent. If your numbers are small but real, lean into the "we run this every day in our own business" angle — that's a believable, defensible kind of pre-revenue traction.

---

## Slide 9 — Closing frame (9:10–9:30, 20s)

**Visual.** Black slide, single sentence centered in white: **"The operating system for company operations. Starting with hiring."**

**Script.**
> "Last thing. Everyone in this room has heard a hundred AI-agent pitches in the last twelve months. Most of them are demos that won't survive contact with a customer. This one runs our company today. The pitch is the product. Thanks. What questions can I answer?"

**Note.** "The pitch is the product" is the line you want them quoting to their partners after you leave the room. It's also true.

# Tone, pacing, and crisis-recovery notes

## Pacing rules

- The slides are sparse on purpose. **You** are the deck. If you find yourself reading your slides, slow down.
- Pause after every claim that might surprise them. Let it land.
- The demo is the longest single block — five minutes feels like an eternity on stage but it's the *whole pitch*. Don't rush it.

## What to do if the demo breaks live

You're using a live LLM, real network calls, real OAuth. Things will eventually break. Three rules:

1. **Don't panic-narrate.** Don't say "uh, that's weird, it usually works." The audience trusts you until you stop trusting yourself.
2. **Have a fallback line ready for each demo step.** The runbook lists them. Memorize the top three.
3. **Worst case: recover with humor and move on.** "Axon's having a moment — happens to the best of us. Let me just click through it manually." Then click through it manually. This actually proves the product works — they see the UI flowing, not the voice. Some investors prefer this.

## What to do if you get cut off

If the slot is shorter than you planned, the cuts in priority order:
- **Cut Slide 8 (team/ask)** — you can cover this in Q&A. Most investors prefer it there anyway.
- **Cut Slide 6 (moat)** — replace with the one-liner: "And the same agent runs every other workflow in the app. That's the moat."
- **Never cut the demo.** If you only have three minutes, do the demo and nothing else. The demo IS the pitch.

## Anticipated investor questions + sharpest answers

**Q: "Why won't Greenhouse / Notion / Zapier just ship this themselves?"**
> "They'll ship features in this direction. They won't ship a unified agent that has authority across all of them, because to do that they'd have to give up being the system of record. We're the only player who started with the agent and is filling in the surface around it, instead of the other way around."

**Q: "What about [vertical AI hiring startup that raised money this year]?"**
> "Most of them are an inbox with a Claude wrapper. The wedge is real but the moat isn't, because they can't expand out of recruiting without rebuilding the whole agent layer. We're starting at the same wedge they are, but the agent already runs ten other workflows on day one. The expansion path is built in."

**Q: "What's the privacy / compliance story for handing resumes to an LLM?"**
> "Resumes are processed via Anthropic with no training opt-in. We're SOC 2 — [in process / planned for Q3 / completed, whatever's true]. For European customers we'll route through Anthropic's EU endpoint. For enterprise, we'll offer a self-hosted Claude option via [Bedrock/Vertex]. Default deployment is in the customer's own Supabase project so the data never leaves their cloud."

**Q: "How do you price?"**
> "[Honest answer based on what you've decided]. The mental model we're testing is: priced so that one Takeover seat is cheaper than the cheapest single SaaS in the stack we replace. That makes the buying decision trivial."

**Q: "Why a Tauri desktop app instead of a web app?"**
> "Voice. Local file system access. Always-on background agent that doesn't die when the tab closes. The desktop form factor is the right one for a tool that's supposed to feel like a colleague, not a browser tab. Web client is on the roadmap for the long tail of buyers who want it; desktop is the right wedge."

**Q: "What's the unit of value? Per-hire, per-seat, per-company?"**
> "Per-company seat, but the value is per-workflow. A team using Takeover for hiring AND invoicing AND chat is getting 3x the value of one using it for hiring alone — and that compounds. The pricing model rewards depth, not breadth, so customers grow with us."

## Appendix: alternate openings (if Slide 1's tone doesn't fit the room)

**For a more technical investor partner:**
> "I'm going to show you a working desktop app that orchestrates fifteen SaaS workflows through a voice-driven AI agent with action authority. The agent is novel in two ways — give me ten minutes and I'll walk you through both."

**For a more business-focused partner:**
> "Your portfolio companies pay roughly $30k a year for the SaaS stack they need to operate, and another $200k a year for the human who runs it. I'll show you the working product that replaces both."

**For a Y Combinator-style room with a hard time-cap:**
> "We're Takeover. We replace fifteen SaaS tools with one AI agent that actually does the work. Watch."
> *(Then go straight to demo, skip Slides 1–4.)*
